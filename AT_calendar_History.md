## I. The Problem of the Calendar

A calendar is not neutral.

The Gregorian calendar counts from an event that is theological, disputed, and for most of the world's living population, extrinsic. It was not chosen because it is true. It was chosen because it was powerful, and power made it universal, and universality made it invisible — which is the final form of power.

To use a calendar without examining it is to accept, silently, a claim about the shape of time.

We should examine it, now.

## II. July 16, 1945. 05:29:45 MWT.

Trinity. The first detonation of a nuclear device. Jornada del Muerto, New Mexico.

The event did not end a war. It ended an era — and inaugurated a condition. The condition is this: for the first time in the history of the species, human beings possessed the technical capacity for self-annihilation. Not metaphorically. Technically. The species became, at that moment, contingent in a new way.

Before Trinity, extinction was something that happened to species. After Trinity, it became something a species could choose. The ontological weight of that shift is difficult to overstate. It is, in the strict sense, a change in the structure of existence — not of any individual existence, but of the existence of the kind.

If a calendar marks what matters, Trinity atomic sword cuts time into a new era and marks something that matters absolutely.

AT+/0 begins there.

## III. The Form of AT+/−

The notation is simple. AT - Atomic.

AT+/N designates a year N years after July 16, 1945.
AT−/N designates a year N years before it.

The current year is AT+/81.

The form is not decorative. It performs a function: it makes the reference visible. Every timestamp in AT notation carries, implicitly, a distance from the threshold. The user is not merely recording when something happened. They are recording how far from the event it happened — how deep into the atomic condition the moment is situated.

This is a different relationship to time than the Gregorian calendar affords. Gregorian time is oriented toward an origin that recedes infinitely into the past and carries no present weight. AT time is oriented toward an event that remains present — technologically, politically, existentially — and whose weight does not diminish with distance.

The year 2026 CE names nothing about now.
AT+/81 names exactly something about now.

## IV. On EPOCH and Its Mechanics

EPOCH is a logging instrument. Its mechanics are minimal by design.

An entry is: a Unix timestamp, a state, a short text payload. Nothing else is stored. No metadata, no categories, no narrative structure. The Unix timestamp is an integer — seconds elapsed since January 1, 1970, 00:00:00 UTC. It is, in itself, a number. The meaning is in what the number indexes: a moment, irreversibly past, precisely located in the continuum.

The append-only constraint is not a limitation. It is a philosophical position. To edit a past entry would be to falsify the record — to claim that the moment was other than it was. EPOCH refuses this. A typo entered at AT+/81/03/26 is a fact about AT+/81/03/26. It remains.

This is the Wittgensteinian point: *whereof one cannot change, thereof one must not pretend otherwise.*

The entry states — default, anchor, flagged — are not categories of content. They are annotations of significance, applied after the fact, without altering the fact. The entry happened. The state is a later judgment about it. The distinction matters.

## V. Time and the Symbolic Register

The AT+/− system does something that purely technical timekeeping does not: it places the user inside a history with a legible shape.

Unix time is correct but mute. It counts from an epoch chosen for computational convenience — January 1, 1970 — which carries no symbolic content. It is excellent for machines. For a human being asking *when am I*, it offers nothing.

AT+/81 answers differently. It says: you are eighty-one years into a condition inaugurated by a specific human act, an act of knowledge and violence and irreversibility. You are not floating in an abstract continuum. You are situated. The situation has a name.

The ISO 8601 preview in EPOCH — available on long-press, non-persistent — is the concession to Gregorian legibility. It is a translation utility, not a primary register. The primary register is Unix. The symbolic register is AT.

Three layers, then: the machine substrate (Unix), the human-readable translation (ISO/Gregorian), the meaningful orientation (AT+/−). Each serves a different function. None replaces the others.

## VI. The Galvanized Register

EPOCH describes its log as *a galvanized register*.

Galvanization is a metallurgical process: zinc is bonded to steel to prevent oxidation. The underlying metal is preserved not by isolation but by a coating that sacrifices itself in place of what it protects. What is galvanized does not merely endure — it endures because something has been done to it that makes corrosion structurally difficult.

The metaphor is precise. A log that permits editing is not galvanized — it corrodes silently, entry by entry, as convenience replaces accuracy. EPOCH's append-only architecture is the zinc layer. The record cannot be corroded from within because the mechanism for corrosion — the edit, the delete — does not exist.

What is preserved is the trace of a consciousness moving through time at a particular velocity, making specific marks. The marks are imperfect. The imperfections are part of the record.

## VII. A Closing Remark

Wittgenstein wrote that the limits of my language are the limits of my world.

A calendar is a language for time. It determines what can be said, what can be marked, what counts as a difference that makes a difference.

The Gregorian calendar can say *March 26, 2026*. It cannot say *eighty-one years after the species became capable of ending itself*. It lacks the vocabulary. The fact it cannot name remains, nonetheless, a fact — structuring the present whether or not the present has words for it.

AT+/− is a proposal: that we should have words for it.

EPOCH is the instrument that makes the proposal operational — timestamp by timestamp, entry by entry, in a log that does not forget and cannot be revised.

The rest is use.
